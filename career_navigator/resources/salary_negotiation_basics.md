# Salary Negotiation Basics for First-Time Negotiators

Most first-gen candidates accept the first offer. This alone often
leaves money on the table -- most employers expect some negotiation and
build room for it into the initial number.

## The core rule
Never give a number first if you can avoid it. If pushed early in the
process ("what are your salary expectations?"), redirect: "I'd love to
learn more about the role and scope before landing on a number -- what
range do you have budgeted for this position?" This isn't evasive, it's
standard and expected.

## When you do have to give a range
Give a range, not a single number, and anchor it slightly above what
you'd actually accept -- e.g. if your real minimum is $65k, a range of
"$70k-$80k" leaves room to land closer to what you actually want after
the inevitable pushback toward the bottom of your stated range.

## After you receive an offer
1. Say thank you, and ask for 24-48 hours to review -- always reasonable,
   never damages the offer.
2. If the offer is below what you hoped for, negotiate once, clearly:
   "I'm excited about this role. Based on [market research / the scope
   we discussed], I was hoping for something closer to $X. Is there
   flexibility here?"
3. If the base salary truly can't move, ask about other levers: signing
   bonus, remote flexibility, an earlier performance review date, extra
   PTO, or professional development budget.

## What NOT to do
- Don't fabricate a competing offer -- it can be checked, and it damages
  trust if discovered.
- Don't negotiate over email if a call is offered -- tone carries better
  live, and it's harder to walk back an awkward written ask.
- Don't apologize for negotiating. A single, clear, polite ask is
  standard practice, not an imposition.
