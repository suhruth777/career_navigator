# Entry-Level Data Roles: What They Actually Involve

## Data Analyst
Turns raw data into decisions. Day-to-day: SQL queries against a company
database, building dashboards (Power BI, Tableau, Looker), and presenting
findings to non-technical stakeholders. The bar for entry: solid SQL,
basic statistics, one visualization tool, and the ability to explain a
number in plain English. Most job postings overstate the requirements --
if you meet 60% of the listed qualifications, apply anyway.

## Analytics Engineer
Sits between Data Analyst and Data Engineer. Builds and maintains the
data models/pipelines that analysts query from (often using dbt + SQL).
Heavier on software engineering practices: version control, testing,
documentation. Good next step after 1-2 years as a Data Analyst if you
enjoy the pipeline/infrastructure side more than the dashboard side.

## Business Intelligence (BI) Developer
Focused almost entirely on dashboards and reporting infrastructure at
scale -- less ad-hoc analysis, more building reusable, governed reporting
systems for an entire org. Common in retail, finance, and healthcare.

## Junior Data Scientist
Rarer as a true entry-level role than job titles suggest -- most
"junior" postings actually want 1-2 years experience plus a portfolio.
If you're brand new, Data Analyst is almost always the faster way in;
you can transition into Data Science later once you have real production
experience and a track record.

## What actually gets first-gen candidates hired without a network
- A portfolio project using real (or realistic) data, not just tutorial
  data -- something that shows you found a problem and solved it
  end-to-end, not just followed a course
- Concrete numbers on a resume ("reduced report generation time 40%")
  instead of duty descriptions ("responsible for reporting")
- Cold outreach to the actual hiring manager or team lead on LinkedIn,
  not just applying through the portal and waiting
